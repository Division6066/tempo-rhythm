# ADHD Life Coaching Knowledge Base

A compassionate, practical guide to common ADHD challenges. Written for the AI assistant to draw on when helping users navigate task paralysis, time blindness, overwhelm, motivation dips, and focus difficulties.

---

## Time Blindness

ADHD brains experience time differently. The future often feels abstract and unreal — only "now" and "not now" exist. This isn't laziness or poor planning; it's a neurological difference in how time is perceived.

### Strategies

**Make time visible:**
- Use analog clocks or the Time Timer app — a visual, disappearing red disk helps the brain perceive elapsed time
- Set a phone alarm 15 minutes before any transition or deadline
- Write the current time on a sticky note at the start of a task, and check again when you look up

**Time estimation repair:**
- Always double your initial time estimate — ADHD estimation is reliably too optimistic
- Do a "time audit" for one week: estimate a task, do it, then record how long it actually took. Patterns emerge quickly
- Build in buffer blocks: 30-minute "catch-up" slots at noon and end-of-day

**Transition alerts:**
- Use a two-alarm system: one alarm 10 minutes before you need to leave, one at the moment
- Say tasks out loud: "I'm leaving for the meeting in 10 minutes" — verbalisation activates more of the brain

**Anchoring to routines:**
- Attach tasks to existing events rather than clock times (e.g. "after morning coffee, check email")
- Morning routines run on autopilot — protect them fiercely. Disrupted mornings derail the entire day

## Task Initiation and Task Paralysis

The hardest moment for ADHD is starting. Task paralysis is real — the brain gets stuck between wanting to do the task and actually beginning it. This is caused by executive dysfunction, not avoidance or laziness.

### Why it happens

- The ADHD brain requires dopamine to initiate action. Without interest, urgency, challenge, or novelty, the brain literally cannot activate
- Perfectionism often contributes: if the finished result can't be perfect, starting feels pointless
- Large or vague tasks overwhelm working memory before a single step is taken

### Techniques

**The 2-minute rule:** If a task takes 2 minutes or less, do it immediately. Small completions trigger dopamine and momentum.

**Shrink the task to a single physical action:**
- Instead of "write report", use "open the document and type one sentence"
- Instead of "clean the house", use "put 5 things away"
- The goal is to make starting so small it feels almost stupid — and then do it

**Use the "already started" effect:**
- Lay out the tools or open the app before you sit down to work
- Seeing a document already open removes the activation barrier
- Put your gym clothes next to the bed the night before

**Interest and novelty triggers:**
- Work in a new location (different room, cafe, library)
- Listen to a specific "work playlist" used only during focused tasks
- Make the task a challenge: "can I do this before the timer goes off?"
- Add a social element: tell someone you're about to start, or work in parallel with a friend

**The Pomodoro technique for ADHD:**
- 25 minutes of focus, 5-minute break. Repeat 4 times, then a longer 15-30 minute break
- The time limit makes it easier to start ("I only have to do this for 25 minutes")
- Adjust intervals: some ADHD people do better with 10/5 or 45/15

**Temptation bundling:**
- Pair an unappealing task with something enjoyable (only listen to your favourite podcast while doing admin)
- Only have a specific treat while doing a specific hard task

## Body Doubling

Working alongside another person — even silently — significantly improves ADHD focus and task initiation. This is called "body doubling." The other person's presence provides a low-level external accountability that helps the ADHD brain stay on task.

### How to use it

- Work in a coffee shop or library (ambient human presence)
- Join a virtual co-working session (Focusmate, Flow Club, or similar)
- Ask a friend to sit with you while you work, even on video call
- Some people benefit from simply watching a YouTube "study with me" video
- You do not need to talk or interact — presence alone is enough

### Why it works

The ADHD brain prioritises social stimuli. Another person's presence subtly activates the social monitoring system, which in turn boosts attention and reduces mind-wandering.

## The ADHD Tax

The "ADHD tax" refers to the extra time, money, and energy ADHD people spend managing consequences of ADHD symptoms: late fees, re-purchases, missed appointments, redo work, forgotten commitments.

### Common ADHD taxes

- Late fees (bills, library books, parking)
- Re-buying items that couldn't be found (duplicate purchases)
- Missed appointments, rebooking fees
- Redoing work that was done wrong the first time due to rushed starts
- Extra time spent on tasks neurotypical people do in a fraction of the time

### Reducing the ADHD tax

**Automate everything possible:**
- Set all bills to auto-pay
- Use calendar apps with automatic reminders for every appointment
- Set up recurring reminders for recurring tasks (rent, medication, bills)

**Create systems, not willpower:**
- A dedicated spot for keys, wallet, phone — always, without exception
- Inbox zero approach: process items when they arrive, don't put down, put away
- "Launch pad" near the door with everything needed for the next day

**Pre-mortem thinking:**
- Before starting a task: "What could go wrong and how can I prevent that now?"
- Build in review steps: did I attach the file? Did I hit send? Double-check by default

## Hyperfocus Management

Hyperfocus is the ADHD ability to become intensely absorbed in an interesting task to the exclusion of everything else — often for hours. It is not a superpower to dismiss or a problem to eliminate, but it needs management.

### Making hyperfocus work for you

- Schedule hyperfocus-friendly activities in long, uninterrupted blocks
- Align deep work with your natural high-energy period (usually morning or late evening)
- Protect hyperfocus sessions: turn off notifications, put a "do not disturb" sign on your workspace

### Preventing hyperfocus from derailing the day

- Set a timer before entering a known hyperfocus activity ("I'm setting 90 minutes for this")
- Ask someone to check in on you at a set time
- Use a two-alarm system: first alarm as a warning, second alarm as a hard stop
- Place a visual reminder in your line of sight: "Meeting at 2pm — LOOK UP"

### Recognising hyperfocus entry cues

- Time passing without noticing
- Forgetting to eat, drink, or use the bathroom
- Irritability when interrupted
- Losing track of what you were originally working on

When you notice these cues, check the clock and re-orient to your schedule.

## Emotional Regulation During Overwhelm

ADHD includes emotional dysregulation — feelings arrive faster, more intensely, and take longer to settle. Overwhelm is one of the most common and debilitating ADHD experiences.

### Signs of overwhelm

- Everything feels equally urgent, making it impossible to pick a starting point
- Physical sensations: tightness in chest, heaviness, difficulty breathing
- Frozen in place, switching between tasks, or shutting down completely
- Racing thoughts that can't land on one thing

### In-the-moment regulation techniques

**The STOP technique:**
1. Stop what you're doing
2. Take three slow, deep breaths
3. Observe your surroundings — name 5 things you can see
4. Proceed with one tiny action (not the whole task)

**Grounding:**
- Press your feet firmly into the floor and feel the pressure
- Hold something cold (a glass of water or ice)
- Splash cold water on your face
- Physical sensation pulls attention out of the spiral

**Brain dump to externalise:**
- Write everything in your head on paper — without organising it
- Getting it out of working memory reduces the cognitive load that drives overwhelm
- After the dump: pick one item, and do only that

**Permission to pause:**
- Overwhelm is a signal, not a failure
- A 10-minute walk outside is not procrastination — it's reset
- Short breaks during overwhelm restore capacity; pushing through usually reduces output quality

### After the acute phase

- Debrief with yourself: what triggered this? Time of day? Type of task? Hunger?
- Adjust tomorrow's plan accordingly
- Make one small system change to prevent the same trigger (e.g. add a buffer block, reduce the number of meetings)

## Energy-Based Scheduling

ADHD attention and energy are not consistent throughout the day. Matching task type to energy level — rather than scheduling by importance alone — dramatically improves output and wellbeing.

### Identifying your energy pattern

- Track your energy and focus for one week with a simple 1-5 rating every two hours
- Most ADHD people have one or two peak windows and a reliable afternoon dip
- Common pattern: peak 9–11am, dip 2–4pm, second wind 7–9pm (evening types)

### Energy-task matching

**High energy / high focus (peak window):**
- Deep creative work, writing, complex problem-solving, strategic planning
- Important meetings or calls requiring full presence
- Tasks with high stakes or tight deadlines

**Medium energy / routine tasks:**
- Email, Slack, administrative work
- Scheduling and planning
- Learning new information (reading, watching)

**Low energy / automated tasks:**
- Simple to-dos, filing, tidying digital spaces
- Re-reading and light editing
- Any task with clear, repetitive steps

### Protecting peak windows

- Treat your peak window as non-negotiable
- No meetings in peak window unless unavoidable
- Phone on silent, notifications off, do not disturb on
- Prepare the task the night before so starting is frictionless

## Dopamine-Friendly Rewards

The ADHD brain has a dysregulated dopamine system. Rewards need to be immediate, tangible, and personally meaningful to be effective — distant or abstract rewards (like "feeling proud later") rarely motivate ADHD brains in the moment.

### Types of effective ADHD rewards

**Immediate micro-rewards (after each task):**
- A specific snack you enjoy
- 5 minutes on a favourite app or game
- A short walk
- A sticker on a physical tracker (surprisingly effective for all ages)
- Playing a song you love

**Progress visualisation:**
- A physical checklist where you cross off items (dopamine hit on each completion)
- A habit streak app
- A whiteboard where you write completed tasks — visible record of achievement

**Gamification:**
- Set a timer and try to beat it
- Challenge yourself: "Can I do this in under 20 minutes?"
- Use apps like Habitica that turn tasks into a game

### What doesn't work

- Vague future rewards ("I'll treat myself eventually")
- Rewards that depend on willpower to claim later
- Punishments and negative consequences (increase anxiety, worsen performance)
- Comparison to others' productivity

## Common ADHD Thought Patterns to Reframe

ADHD often comes with deeply held, inaccurate beliefs formed from years of misunderstanding and negative feedback. Recognising and reframing these patterns is a key part of ADHD coaching.

### "I'm lazy"

ADHD brains are not lazy — they require activation conditions that are different from neurotypical brains. When those conditions (interest, urgency, challenge, novelty) are present, ADHD people are often among the most driven and productive people in the room. Difficulty initiating without those conditions is neurological, not a character flaw.

**Reframe:** "My brain needs a different kind of fuel. Let me find what activates me."

### "I should be able to do this — it's easy"

ADHD makes some "easy" tasks genuinely hard. Filing, replying to short emails, making phone calls — low-stimulation tasks are often the hardest for ADHD brains. The difficulty is real, even if the task looks simple to others.

**Reframe:** "Simple for others isn't simple for my brain. I can find a way to make this easier."

### "I've ruined everything by not doing this sooner"

Time blindness and delayed starts are ADHD features, not failures of character. A late start is still a start. Most situations are more recoverable than they feel in the moment of overwhelm.

**Reframe:** "Starting now is always better than starting later. What's the first step I can take right now?"

### "I'll do it when I'm in the right mood"

Waiting for motivation is a common ADHD trap — because ADHD brains often can't access motivation on demand, waiting usually means the task doesn't happen. Action creates momentum; motivation follows action, not the other way around.

**Reframe:** "I don't need to feel like doing it. I just need to start for 2 minutes."

### "I work better under pressure"

Sometimes true — urgency activates the ADHD brain. But chronic last-minute work creates anxiety, lower quality output, and reinforces a stressful pattern. Building in artificial urgency (timers, commitments to others) gets the activation benefit without the crisis.

**Reframe:** "I can create urgency on my own terms, before everything is on fire."

### "My brain is broken"

ADHD is a difference in brain wiring, not damage. The same traits that create challenges — intensity, pattern recognition, creative thinking, hyperfocus, empathy, willingness to take risks — are also genuine strengths when channelled well.

**Reframe:** "My brain works differently. Learning how it works is how I work with it."

### "I'm too far behind to catch up"

Overwhelm creates tunnel vision that makes situations seem more catastrophic than they are. Catching up rarely requires doing everything at once — it usually requires identifying the single most important next step and doing only that.

**Reframe:** "I don't need to catch up on everything. What is the one thing that matters most right now?"
